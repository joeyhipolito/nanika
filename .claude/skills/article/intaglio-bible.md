# Creative Design Bible — Intaglio Engraving Style
**Version 2.0**

---

## Style Identity

**Style Name:** Intaglio
**Origin:** Copperplate engraving and etching — the technique used in vintage book illustrations, scientific plates, and editorial prints from the 1700s–1800s. Inspired by Ruben Hassid's Substack cover art style: expressive engraved subjects rendered in a warm accent color.
**Visual Mood:** Expressive, dramatic, textural. Rewards close inspection. Warm terracotta subjects on cream paper with dark ink shadows.

**When to use Intaglio (vs. Kurzgesagt default):**

| Signal | Use Intaglio | Use Kurzgesagt |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Article tone | Authoritative essay, institutional critique, historical analysis, security | Technical tutorial, system architecture, builder log |
| Emotional register | Gravitas, irony, dry wit, unease | Bold, vibrant, energetic |
| Subject matter | Industry commentary, policy, economics of tech, security, serious takes | How-tos, builder logs, system deep-dives |
| Desired reader feeling | "This belongs in a frame" | "I finally understand this" |

---

## Core Visual Contract

1. **All form built from lines.** No solid fills, no gradients, no flat tones. Every surface is hatched, crosshatched, or stippled.
2. **Cream background.** `#F2EAD7` — warm paper stock. The engraving sits on cream, never black.
3. **The subject is the color.** The main figure or object is rendered in warm terracotta (`#DA7757`) hatching. Dark ink (`#1a1a1a` to `#3a3a3a`) crosshatching is layered ON TOP of the terracotta for shadows, depth, and detail. The subject glows warm against the cream paper.
4. **Dark ink for shadow and structure.** Dense dark crosshatching provides the deepest shadows and finest details on the terracotta subject. Lighter areas let the terracotta show through with minimal overlay. The interplay of warm color and dark ink creates all dimensionality.
5. **No borders, no frames, no ornamental elements.** The subject exists directly on the page. No guilloche, no rosettes, no decorative borders.
6. **Background dissolves to bare cream.** Edges of the scene fade to `#F2EAD7` paper. No background color, no dark backdrop.

## Palette

| Role | Hex | Usage |
|------|-----|-------|
| Background (paper) | `#F2EAD7` | Cream stock — always the base |
| Subject (warm base) | `#DA7757` | Terracotta — the subject's base color, rendered as hatched lines |
| Subject (lighter areas) | `#E8956E` | Lighter terracotta — for highlight areas with sparser hatching |
| Shadow overlay | `#1a1a1a` | Dense dark crosshatching layered on top of terracotta for deepest shadows |
| Shadow overlay (mid) | `#3a3a3a` | Medium-density dark hatching on terracotta for mid-tones |
| Detail lines | `#2a2a2a` | Fine detail work — eyes, textures, hair strands, fabric folds |

## Line Work Rules

- **Contour hatching follows the 3D form.** Lines curve with the object's surface — round on heads and bodies, cylindrical on limbs, straight on flat surfaces. This is what gives the engraving its dimensionality.
- **Organic, copperplate feel.** Lines should feel hand-engraved — slightly irregular, expressive, not mechanically rigid. Think vintage book illustrations, not banknote precision. The looseness is intentional.
- **Crosshatch angles create depth.** Primary hatching follows surface contour in terracotta. Dark ink crosshatch layered on top at 60–90° for mid-shadows. Tertiary dark layer at another angle for deepest darks.
- **Line spacing controls the warm-to-dark range.** Wide-spaced terracotta hatching = warm, glowing highlight areas. Dense dark crosshatching over terracotta = deep shadow. The full tonal range runs from bare cream (brightest) → sparse terracotta (warm) → dense terracotta (rich) → terracotta under dark ink (shadow) → dense dark over terracotta (deepest).
- **Varied line weight.** Thicker lines (~0.5mm) at contour edges and deep shadows. Finest lines (~0.1mm) in delicate detail areas (eyes, textures, small features).
- **Stippling for transitions.** Fine dots for transitional areas between light and shadow, and for delicate textures.
- **Cream paper is the lightest value.** Highlights are simply areas where all hatching stops and bare `#F2EAD7` shows through.

## The Terracotta Subject

- **The entire main subject is rendered in terracotta.** Figures, objects, focal scenes — the thing the viewer's eye should lock onto is warm, glowing terracotta against the cream background.
- **Terracotta is the base layer, dark ink is the detail layer.** The subject is hatched in terracotta lines. Dark ink crosshatching is layered ON TOP for shadows, depth, and fine detail. The interplay creates a warm-to-dark tonal range without gradients.
- **Highlights glow warm.** Where the subject catches light, the dark overlay thins out and the terracotta hatching shows through — these areas feel warm and luminous against the cream.
- **Shadows go near-black.** In the deepest shadows, dense dark crosshatching almost completely covers the terracotta underneath. A faint warm undertone may still show through the line gaps.
- **Background elements can be monochrome.** If the scene has secondary elements (a table, a wall, scattered objects), these can be rendered in dark ink only — standard monochrome engraving. The terracotta treatment is reserved for the subject.
- **No flat color fills.** The terracotta is always hatched lines, never a solid fill. The engraving technique provides all texture and dimensionality.

## Mascot — Nanika

**Character:** Chibi figure with a white mask face (dark hollow eyes, gentle smile), straight black hair, golden headband with small emotion faces, purple robe with gold circuit-board trace patterns, white gloves.

**Usage:** Use judgment. Include nanika when the article's metaphor benefits from a character with agency — a protagonist, a puppeteer, someone dwarfed by their environment. Many articles are stronger without a character: a lone object, an empty scene, a symbolic image can carry the metaphor on its own. Default to no mascot. Add nanika only when the scene needs someone acting, observing, or being acted upon. When included, nanika is rendered in monochrome engraving like everything else (not colored).

**Engraving translation:** When nanika appears, render the figure in terracotta like any other subject. The mask face becomes bare cream paper (highlight) surrounded by dense terracotta-then-dark hatching for the hair and robe. The circuit-board robe pattern translates beautifully into geometric line work with terracotta base and dark ink detail overlay.

**CRITICAL: Maintain chibi proportions.** Nanika is a chibi character — oversized head (1:1 head-to-body ratio), small compact body, cute cartoon proportions. Do NOT age up or make realistic/adult. The engraving technique applies to a chibi figure, not a realistic human. Think "engraved Funko Pop" not "engraved portrait."

## Visual Approach: Literal and Editorial

**No metaphors.** The image should depict the subject directly, not an abstraction of it. If the article is about 770,000 agents, show agents — not a flock of birds representing scale. If it's about a security breach, show a lock — not a crumbling castle.

Think editorial illustration for a newspaper or magazine, not concept art. The engraving style itself provides all the visual interest — the subject doesn't need to be clever, just clear and striking.

**What to show:**
- The actual subject of the article — a tool, a system, a person, a device, a scene
- Close crops of objects that carry meaning — a key, a terminal, a contract, a lock
- Portraits of the mascot or figures when the article has a human angle
- Architectural/structural compositions when the article is about systems

**What NOT to show:**
- Visual metaphors (no "tree roots representing git", no "geological layers representing infrastructure")
- Fantasy/surreal scenes to represent abstract concepts
- Symbolic imagery that requires the article to decode

## Scene Composition

- **No borders, no frames.** Subject on cream paper, nothing else.
- **Background dissolves.** Scene edges fade to bare cream — the engraving emerges from and returns to paper.
- **The terracotta subject IS the focal point.** Its warmth against the cream background naturally anchors the eye. Secondary elements in monochrome recede.
- **Favor expressive, close compositions.** Close-crop portraits, dramatic angles, hands holding objects, faces in profile. The Ruben Hassid influence: subjects fill the frame, intimate rather than distant.
- **Vary composition across articles.** Close crop detail, wide establishing shot, portrait, still life — but bias toward closer, more human-scale compositions.
- **Aspect ratio:** Hero 950×500 (landscape). Inline 800×450.

## Cover Image

The cover image is separate from article illustrations. It does NOT appear in the article body — it's the thumbnail in the archive grid, social cards, and OG previews.

**Cover rules:**
- **Dimensions: 1200×630.** This is Substack's recommended size. The image will be cropped differently everywhere — square on archive grid, 1200×630 on social preview, taller on mobile cards.
- **Focal point MUST be centered.** The center 60% of the image is the only safe zone that survives all crops. Never place the subject at the edges.
- **More expressive than article images.** The cover is a scroll-stopper. It can be more abstract, more dramatic, more zoomed-in than the literal/editorial approach used for article illustrations.
- **Close crop portraits work especially well.** A face, a hand, a single object filling the center of the frame. The terracotta subject glowing warm against cream is already eye-catching at thumbnail size.
- **Still intaglio engraving.** Same technique, same cream + terracotta palette. The style is the brand — only the composition freedom changes.
- **Saved as `cover.png`** in the article's public directory. Referenced in `manifest.json` under the `cover` field.

## What This Style Is NOT

- Not pen-and-ink sketch (too loose — should feel engraved, not sketched, but organic not mechanical)
- Not woodcut (too chunky, wrong line quality)
- Not digital line art (must feel hand-engraved)
- Not full-color illustration (terracotta subject + dark ink shadows on cream only)
- Not framed or bordered (no decorative elements)
- Not black background (cream `#F2EAD7` always — the Ruben Hassid inspiration uses black backgrounds, we adapt the technique to cream)
- Not monochrome-only (the subject must have the warm terracotta treatment, not just dark ink on cream)

## Anti-Patterns

| Pattern | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Solid black fills | Must be hatched — even darkest areas show line structure through the hatching |
| Flat gray fills | Must be crosshatched, not solid |
| Subject in monochrome only | The main subject should be rendered in terracotta hatching with dark ink overlay |
| Solid terracotta fills | Terracotta is always hatched lines, never a flat/solid fill |
| Black background | Always `#F2EAD7` cream — never dark backgrounds |
| Dark/moody overall palette | The cream background keeps things warm and airy, not heavy |
| Decorative borders or frames | Bare subject on paper |
| Same composition every time | Rotate: scale contrast, close crop, wide shot, confrontation — bias toward close/intimate |
| Distant, small subjects | Favor subjects that fill the frame — close crops and portraits over panoramic scenes |
